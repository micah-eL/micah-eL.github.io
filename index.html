About

The Spring 2022 semester marked the start of my fourth year at Simon Fraser University majoring in Computer Science. It also marked my first semester at SFU since retiring from the SFU Men's Varsity Swim team.

Through the first two years of my undergraduate degree, I spent a great deal of energy trying to balance varsity athletics with a full CS course load. Although some may argue, I believe this balancing act is often a zero-sum game. As such, I wasn’t able to perform at my full potential in both my athletic and academic pursuits.
However, going into my third year, I stopped training as heavily - in large part because of an issue between administration and the coaching staff, but also because I found that I had become much more excited and driven by opportunities presenting themselves outside the pool. As that 2020/21 school year progressed, I found myself spending less and less time in the pool, which gave me more time than ever to invest in my academics. This began to show in my grades and in the confidence in my academic abilities. This all culminated in landing an SDE internship with FORM and finally making the decision to retire from swimming.

May 2021, I started my internship at FORM and my life without swimming. Of course, without the countless hours spent in the pool or weight room, I found that I had so much more time and energy. No more living at the pool. No more struggling to find time to finish schoolwork. And my favorite - no more getting up at 5:00am to jump into cold pools. As such, I started filling every free hour of my day up learning new things and just exploring my other interests further, which included making this website!
And now I am in the midst of my 4th year of classes; my remaining upper division courses will reflect my Computer Systems and Information Systems concentrations. Outside of classes, I coach with Hyack Swim Club and I'm continuously looking to learn new things, whether that be by working on side projects or reading through papers. I'm also currently looking for a Fall 2022 internship, so I'd love to hear about any opportunities!

I hope you enjoy looking through my website and the projects I’ve worked on. Of course, now that I have more time and energy than I know what to do with, I’ll be continuously adding new projects I work on, so I hope you come back to check those out as well.

Terminal texting

My recent interest in the lower level aspects of computer science stems from my interest in learning how things work. But not just a superficial understanding, but a deep - usually all the way down to hardware - understanding of what exactly is going on. Often times this can be to my detriment because sometimes that formula they give you in your Numerical Analysis or Linear Algebra class is meant to be memorized and used, not studied for hours on end! But regardless, this project was another case of me going down the rabbit hole, this time with computer networking. I ended up picking up Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach, 6th edition, and after having read a few chapters, I'd highly recommend reading through at least the first chapter. Some of the information presented really made me re-evaluate how much I thought I knew about things like the Internet and the World Wide Web. This application was actually inpsired by the example Python application in chapter 2.7.2 of this textbook.

These two C programs make use of Unix‘s networking API and they just follow a basic TCP connection flow. Server-side:

getaddrinfo() -> socket() -> bind() -> listen() and accept() -> fork() -> recv() and send() -> close() child process -> repeat from listen() and accept()

Client-side:

getaddrinfo() -> socket() -> connect() -> send() and recv() -> close() and shutdown()

One thing that tripped me up at first was getaddrinfo() returning a linked list. It does this because a host could have multiple ways to contact to it, for example, the network host could be accessible over both a TCP and UDP connection.
And no, 192.168.0.5 isn't really my IP address, I hope you knew that without having to read a computer networking textbook.
You can check this project out on my GitHub.

One article that I really enjoyed reading was this Stack Overflow article. I was originally intrigued by this stuff after spending some time talking with some of the senior engineers at FORM about the back end infrastructure they had set up when the company was just getting started, and how it has evolved into what it is today. In fact, this was one of my main motivations for my xSwimTeam iOS app + REST API project. Seeing how everything is connected in the big picture is just so incredibly satisfying, but it also serves as a reminder that these services that seem simple at first actually have a complex but efficient design. For example, caching seems simple in theory, but there are a few things you might not think about if you've never actually implemented it. In a mobile app we can cache network responses using key-value storage, where keys are the URLs, and the values are the JSON responses. This seems pretty straightforward, but now remember that some network requests, like network requests that are paginated, won’t have unique URLs (the "nextCursor" string used to represent the current page is usually sent in the request body), meaning you can’t just use the URL as the cache key. This means that basically any URL that specifies uniqueness to the API in their request body needs to specify a unique cache key, usually by extracting the appropriate parameter (ex. nextCursor) from the request body and into the URL.
